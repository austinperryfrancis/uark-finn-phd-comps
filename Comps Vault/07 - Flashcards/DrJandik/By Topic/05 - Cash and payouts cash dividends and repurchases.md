# Week 5 - Cash and payouts: cash, dividends, and repurchases

## Front
How does Week 5 explain why cash and payout policies changed over time?

## Back

### Narrative
This section links liquidity and payout policy to precautionary motives, taxes, and changing public-firm composition. Bates et al. show that cash became more valuable over time because firms faced better growth opportunities, more volatility, more competition, and greater financing frictions. Faulkender et al. separate foreign tax incentives from precautionary savings and show that both matter for the cash buildup, with location of cash central to interpretation. Kahle and Stulz show that payouts remain high because repurchases dominate and public firms increasingly differ from older public-firm populations. Li et al. add that payout policy can respond to product-market regulation and price constraints.

### Main Findings
- The marginal value of cash rose substantially from the 1980s to the 2000s.
- Foreign tax incentives help explain offshore cash, but precautionary savings remains central.
- Repurchases, not dividends alone, drive high modern payout levels.
- Product-market structure and price ceilings can shape payout decisions.

### Exam Use
Use this week to connect liquidity and payout decisions to financing frictions, taxes, firm composition, and product-market constraints.
