# Week 2 - Capital structure: determinants and targets, taxes, and information asymmetry

## Front
What is the Week 2 narrative on why firms choose capital structure and how stable those choices are?

## Back

### Narrative
This section presents capital structure as the outcome of multiple imperfect forces rather than one universal target. Bharath et al. support the pecking-order channel by showing that firms facing more adverse selection rely more on debt when funding deficits arise. DeAngelo and Roll push against simple stable-target stories by showing that leverage ratios and rankings are much less persistent over long horizons than static tradeoff intuition suggests. Oztekin broadens the setting internationally and shows that reliable determinants exist, but institutions affect both leverage levels and adjustment speeds. Graham et al. bring in a managerial input problem: firms often use tax rates that are easy to observe but wrong for decision-making, which can distort financing and investment.

### Main Findings
- Information asymmetry increases reliance on debt, especially when adverse selection is severe.
- Long-run leverage stability is unusual; firms migrate substantially across leverage regimes.
- Size, tangibility, profitability, industry leverage, inflation, and institutions are reliable determinants across countries.
- Using GAAP or statutory tax rates instead of marginal tax rates can create meaningful capital-structure inefficiency.

### Exam Use
Use this week to avoid one-theory answers. Capital structure reflects taxes, adverse selection, adjustment frictions, institutional quality, and managerial measurement choices.
