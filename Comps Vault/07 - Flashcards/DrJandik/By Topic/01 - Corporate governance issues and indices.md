# Week 1 - Corporate governance: issues and indices

## Front
How does the Week 1 governance literature explain why governance institutions, legal protections, and firm-level provisions matter?

## Back

### Narrative
This section frames corporate governance as a system of constraints on managerial private benefits. Denis and McConnell set the broad international view: governance mechanisms differ across countries, and firm-level boards, ownership, compensation, and takeover discipline only work inside a broader legal and institutional environment. Djankov et al. sharpen that idea by showing that law matters most when it gives minority shareholders practical protection against self-dealing. Bebchuk et al. then move inside U.S. firms and show that not every governance provision matters equally; a small set of entrenchment provisions carries most of the valuation and return signal. Coles et al. complicate formal independence by showing that directors appointed after the CEO arrives can be formally independent but less effective monitors.

### Main Findings
- Investor protection and legal enforcement shape ownership, market development, and governance effectiveness.
- Anti-self-dealing rules predict stronger stock market development and lower private benefits of control.
- A compact entrenchment index explains more than broad governance checklists.
- Board independence is weaker when independent directors are co-opted by the CEO appointment process.

### Exam Use
Use this week to argue that governance is not one mechanism. Legal institutions, charter provisions, and board composition interact, and measures that look broad or formal can hide the real monitoring margin.
