# Week 4 - Debt contracts: covenants, social capital, lender hold-up, and creditor control

## Front
What is the Week 4 narrative on debt contracting and creditor governance?

## Back

### Narrative
This section treats debt contracts as governance tools that respond to information, trust, and control rights. Hasan et al. show that local social capital reduces lender concerns about opportunism, lowering spreads and loosening contract terms. Prilmeier shows that lending relationships change covenant design over time as lenders learn, with covenant tightness declining in relationship duration. Bird et al. show that private borrowers can voluntarily disclose information to reduce incumbent-lender hold-up and attract outside finance. Ersahin et al. then show what happens after control rights shift: covenant violations give creditors influence over internal resource allocation.

### Main Findings
- High-social-capital borrowers receive cheaper and less restrictive debt.
- Covenants relax as relationship lenders learn about opaque borrowers.
- Voluntary disclosure reduces lender hold-up and improves loan access for private firms.
- Covenant violations lead to cuts in employment, investment, and weak establishments, especially where creditor expertise is high.

### Exam Use
Use this week to argue that debt is active governance. Covenants, disclosure, relationships, and social capital determine who has control and how resources are reallocated.
