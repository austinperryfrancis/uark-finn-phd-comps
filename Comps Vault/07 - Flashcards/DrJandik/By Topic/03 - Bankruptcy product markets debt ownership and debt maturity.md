# Week 3 - Capital structure: bankruptcy, product markets, debt ownership, and debt maturity

## Front
How does Week 3 connect financial distress, product-market threats, governance, and debt maturity?

## Back

### Narrative
This section shifts from leverage levels to the consequences and structure of debt. Bris et al. show that bankruptcy costs are heterogeneous and depend heavily on procedure, measurement, and asset preservation. Parise shows that firms respond before distress by lengthening debt maturity when future product-market competition threatens cash flows, reducing rollover risk without necessarily changing leverage. Bharath and Hertzel show that firms substitute between creditor governance and other external governance mechanisms, choosing bank debt and covenant strictness based on product-market and takeover discipline. Friedrich and Zator complete the narrative by showing that high leverage reduces real flexibility when demand shocks hit.

### Main Findings
- Bankruptcy costs vary widely, and Chapter 11 can preserve assets better than Chapter 7.
- Airlines exposed to likely low-cost carrier entry lengthen debt maturity before entry occurs.
- External governance pressure reduces demand for bank monitoring; weaker takeover discipline increases it.
- High leverage limits firms' ability to redirect sales and adapt after negative demand shocks.

### Exam Use
Use this week to connect debt policy to real-side risk. Debt maturity, creditor control, and leverage all shape how firms survive competition, distress, and demand shocks.
