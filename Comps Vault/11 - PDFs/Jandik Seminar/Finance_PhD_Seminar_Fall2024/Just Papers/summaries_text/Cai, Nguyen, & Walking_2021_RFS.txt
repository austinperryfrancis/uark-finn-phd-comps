**Summary**  
This study examines the role of professional networks in director appointments across 9,801 instances from 2003–2014. A striking 69% of new directors had professional ties to incumbent board members, despite connected individuals representing only 13% of the potential candidate pool. These connections facilitate diversity, with boards 33% more likely to appoint female directors to all-male boards and 19–34% more likely to add directors with new skills or industry expertise. Complex firms and those in competitive environments benefited most from connected appointments, experiencing higher market returns (+0.91–1.5% abnormal returns) and shareholder votes, as connections reduced coordination costs and information asymmetry. However, appointments linked to the CEO led to negative market reactions (-3.3% returns) and lower votes, reflecting cronyism concerns. The analysis used instrumental variables (network shocks from deaths/mergers) to address endogeneity, confirming causality.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Prevalence of Connections**: 69% of director appointees had professional ties to incumbents, despite connected candidates constituting just 13% of the talent pool. Connected candidates had 2.04× higher odds of appointment (odds ratio *e*<sup>0.712</sup>).  
- **Context-Dependent Value**: Complex firms (e.g., larger boards, high industry fluidity) saw significantly positive market reactions (+1.5% returns) and shareholder support for connected directors, while CEO-connected appointments reduced returns (-3.3%).  
- **Diversity & Agency Trade-offs**: Connections improved gender diversity (33% higher odds for female appointments) and skill diversity, but ties to CEOs correlated with weaker governance (e.g., lower votes, negative returns).
