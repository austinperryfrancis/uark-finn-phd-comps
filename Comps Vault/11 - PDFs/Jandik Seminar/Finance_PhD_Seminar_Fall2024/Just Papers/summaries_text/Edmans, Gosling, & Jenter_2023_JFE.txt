**Summary:**  
The study investigates CEO compensation through surveys of directors and institutional investors in the UK. It finds that boards prioritize avoiding controversy over strict shareholder value maximization, with 67% of directors admitting they would sacrifice shareholder value to prevent backlash from investors, employees, or the public. This leads to lower pay levels and homogenized pay structures. Directors and investors disagree on key objectives: directors emphasize attracting/retaining CEOs (labor market constraints), while investors focus on incentive alignment. Financial incentives (e.g., bonuses, equity) are secondary to intrinsic motivation and personal reputation as CEO motivators. However, performance-linked pay remains critical for *ex-post recognition* of contributions, even when CEOs hold substantial equity. Pay adjustments reflect fairness concerns (e.g., peer benchmarking, internal equity) more than risk-sharing or traditional incentive theory.  

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Controversy avoidance drives pay decisions:** 67% of directors sacrifice shareholder value to avoid disputes, with 77% forced to reduce pay levels and 72% adopting suboptimal structures due to investor/proxy advisor pressures.  
- **Non-financial motivators dominate:** Intrinsic drive (directors: 92%, investors: 91%) and reputation (directors: 91%, investors: 96%) outweigh financial incentives, yet performance-linked pay persists to validate CEO contributions fairly.  
- **Fairness, not risk-sharing, explains pay practices:** Relative performance evaluation is rare (63% of directors avoid it) due to fairness norms (CEOs "share" industry gains/losses with stakeholders) and peer-group practicality challenges.
