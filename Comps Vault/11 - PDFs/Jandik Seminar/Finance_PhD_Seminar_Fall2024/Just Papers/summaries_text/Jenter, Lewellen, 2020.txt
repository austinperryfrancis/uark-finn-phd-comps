**Summary:**  
The study "Performance-Induced CEO Turnover" by Jenter and Lewellen redefines CEO turnover analysis by introducing "performance-induced turnover," which occurs when poor performance triggers a departure. Using two empirical approaches—a probit model with performance deciles and a two-probit model—the authors estimate that 38% to 55% of turnovers are performance-induced, exceeding traditional forced turnover classifications. They find that even "voluntary" exits correlate with poor performance, suggesting prior studies understate turnover-performance links. Notably, performance-induced turnover declines more slowly with tenure than forced turnover, challenging theories of CEO entrenchment. The results also conflict with Bayesian learning models that assume stable CEO ability, instead implying boards treat CEO ability (or match quality) as subject to frequent shocks, weighting recent performance more heavily than older data.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **High Prevalence of Performance-Driven Exits:** 38%–55% of CEO turnovers are performance-induced, significantly higher than forced turnover rates (2–6% for poor vs. top performers).  
- **Methodological Insight:** The two-probit model, accounting for both performance-driven and unrelated turnovers, shows recent performance (e.g., coefficients of −0.298 for 3-year returns) matters more than older performance.  
- **Governance Dynamics:** Turnover sensitivity to performance persists late into tenure (e.g., 5.3% rate at 17+ years), suggesting boards react to evolving ability or external pressures rather than entrenched entrenchment.
