**Summary:**  
This study investigates the determinants of capital structure across 37 countries, identifying firm size, asset tangibility, industry leverage, profitability, and inflation as consistently significant factors. Larger firms and those with more tangible assets or higher industry leverage tend to have higher debt ratios, while greater profitability and inflation correlate with lower leverage. The analysis employs a dynamic panel model, accounting for adjustment costs and unobserved heterogeneity, revealing an average adjustment speed of 21% toward target leverage (implying a three-year half-life). Institutional quality significantly influences financing decisions: stronger legal and financial institutions (e.g., efficient bankruptcy processes, creditor protection) accelerate leverage adjustments. Conversely, environments with robust shareholder rights, transparency, and contract enforcement are associated with lower debt levels due to reduced equity costs and agency conflicts.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Reliable Determinants of Leverage:**  
  - **Positive:** Firm size (+), Tangibility (+), Industry leverage (+).  
  - **Negative:** Profitability (−), Inflation (−).  

- **Adjustment Speed:**  
  - Firms adjust toward target leverage at 21% annually, faster in countries with high-quality institutions (e.g., efficient bankruptcy systems boost speed by 3% per standard deviation improvement).  

- **Institutional Impact:**  
  - Strong creditor rights and bankruptcy efficiency increase leverage.  
  - Shareholder protection, transparency, and enforcement standards reduce leverage but improve adjustment dynamics.
