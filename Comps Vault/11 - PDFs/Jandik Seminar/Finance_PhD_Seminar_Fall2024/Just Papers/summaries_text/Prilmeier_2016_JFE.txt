**Summary:**  
This study investigates how lending relationships influence covenant structures in corporate loans, analyzing 7,924 loans from 1995–2008. The findings reveal that covenant tightness (the probability of breaching covenants) decreases as the duration of the lender-borrower relationship lengthens, particularly for opaque borrowers (e.g., small, unrated firms). This aligns with theories suggesting reduced information asymmetry over time (Gârleanu and Zwiebel, 2009). In contrast, covenant intensity (number of covenants) follows an inverted U-shaped relationship with relationship intensity: medium-intensity relationships have the most covenants, while both low- and high-intensity relationships use fewer. The decline in covenants for exclusive (high-intensity) relationships may reflect reduced free-riding or lower monitoring costs. Instrumental variable analyses, using geographic proximity to syndication-active banks, support causal interpretations, and results hold after robustness checks.

**Key Takeaways:**  
1. **Covenant tightness relaxes with relationship duration**: A 1% increase in relationship duration reduces tightness by ~0.01 (coefficients: −0.0095 to −0.0129, *p* < 0.01), especially for opaque borrowers (e.g., −0.0112 for small firms, *p* < 0.01).  
2. **Covenant intensity peaks in medium-intensity relationships**: Loans with medium relationship intensity (30–70% of prior loans from the same lender) include 8% more covenants than low-intensity loans, but high-intensity (≥70%) loans have 4% fewer than medium (coefficients: −0.073 for low vs. medium, *p* < 0.01; −0.033 for high vs. medium, *p* < 0.05).  
3. **Borrower bargaining power moderates covenant use**: Rated firms in exclusive relationships reduce covenant intensity by 6–7% (*p* < 0.05), suggesting covenants are less critical when alternative financing (e.g., public debt) is available.
