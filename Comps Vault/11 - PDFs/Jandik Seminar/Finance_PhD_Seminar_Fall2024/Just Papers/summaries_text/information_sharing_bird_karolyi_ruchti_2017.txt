**Summary:**  
The study examines how private firms mitigate lender holdup—where incumbent lenders exploit information asymmetry to charge higher rates—by voluntarily sharing financial information in loan agreements. Using data from the Loan Pricing Corporation (LPC), the authors find that disclosing firms switch lenders 16% more frequently, secure loans with 4% lower spreads, and obtain 8% larger loan amounts. These outcomes stem from increased lender competition as disclosures reduce information gaps, weakening incumbents' pricing power. The analysis of firms transitioning to IPOs reveals that disclosure likelihood rises as firms approach going public, where mandatory reporting diminishes proprietary costs. Instrumental variable methods address unobserved borrower quality bias, suggesting lower-quality firms may benefit more from disclosure. Despite concerns about aiding competitors, proprietary costs (e.g., R&D intensity, industry competition) deter disclosure, though benefits often outweigh these costs.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Increased Lender Competition:** Voluntary disclosure correlates with a **16% higher lender-switching rate** and larger, more diverse syndicates, indicating reduced information asymmetry.  
- **Improved Loan Terms:** Disclosing firms secure **4% lower interest spreads** and **8% higher loan amounts**, reflecting enhanced bargaining power.  
- **Proprietary Costs vs. IPO Timing:** Firms near an IPO disclose more (costs decline as mandatory reporting looms), while R&D-intensive or concentrated industries disclose less due to competitive risks. Coefficients for R&D intensity (−13.55) and industry competition (−10.75 HHI) highlight these trade-offs.
