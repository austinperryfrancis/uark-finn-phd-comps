**Summary**  
This research paper investigates how human capital transferability (HCT) between industries influences corporate diversification through acquisitions. Using U.S. Census and labor mobility data, the authors find that firms are more likely to acquire companies in industries with high HCT—measured by cross-industry worker transitions. These diversifying acquisitions yield 18% higher labor productivity gains compared to low-HCT deals and are less likely to be divested. The benefits stem from efficient internal labor markets: high-HCT mergers retain more skilled workers and redeploy them across divisions, particularly in industries with uncorrelated shocks. Stricter enforcement of noncompete agreements amplifies these effects, as firms in such states face higher external hiring costs, making internal reallocation more valuable. Worker-level data confirm that high-HCT mergers increase retention and cross-industry transfers, underpinning productivity gains.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **HCT Drives Diversification**: Industry pairs with 1 standard deviation higher HCT see a 58% increase in diversifying deal frequency, surpassing product-market linkages.  
- **Productivity Boost**: High-HCT acquisitions improve labor productivity by 18%, with effects magnified in states enforcing noncompete agreements (e.g., 30% higher productivity using high-skill HCT measures).  
- **Worker Dynamics**: High-HCT deals retain 2.2% more skilled workers (vs. losses in low-HCT deals) and have 10–12% higher internal job transfers, enabling knowledge sharing and flexibility.
