**Summary**  
The research paper investigates how international trade networks propagate merger waves across countries from 1989 to 2016. Using global trade and M&A data, the authors find that trade-weighted merger activity in a country’s trading partners significantly predicts its likelihood of entering a cross-border or domestic merger wave, even after controlling for lagged domestic activity. For example, a 25th-to-75th percentile increase in trade-connected merger activity raises the probability of a "high M&A state" by 23–30%. Instrumental variable analyses (e.g., import tariff cuts and sanctions) confirm causality, showing trade acts as a channel for spillovers. The study also highlights that countries central in trade networks (e.g., the U.S., Germany) are more sensitive to global merger waves and that cultural/institutional barriers (e.g., in China, Russia) weaken propagation. Firm-level evidence suggests trade-driven learning and exposure underpin these dynamics, with cultural distance and property rights further moderating effects.  

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Trade links drive merger waves**: Trade-weighted merger activity in partner countries (coefficients: 16.549 for imports, 17.289 for exports) strongly predicts domestic and cross-border waves, emphasizing trade as a conduit for economic spillovers.  
- **Causal validation**: Instrumental variables (tariff cuts, sanctions) confirm trade’s role in propagating mergers, with placebo tests ruling out spurious correlations.  
- **Network centrality matters**: Central trade hubs (e.g., U.S., Germany) disproportionately amplify global merger activity, while cultural/institutional barriers (e.g., high power distance, weak property rights) dampen propagation.
