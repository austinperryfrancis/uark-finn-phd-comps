**Summary**  
This study investigates how cap-and-trade policies impact bank lending to greenhouse gas (GHG)-emitting firms, analyzing California’s 2013 program and the federal Waxman-Markey bill. Using regulatory and emissions data, the authors find that affected firms faced shorter loan maturities (5 months under California’s policy, up to 7 months under Waxman-Markey), reduced access to term loans (25 percentage point decline), higher interest rates (up to 1.7 percentage points for private firms), and increased participation of shadow banks in lending syndicates. These effects were concentrated among private firms, which are less emissions-efficient and more financially constrained than public firms. Post-policy, private firms also experienced reduced profitability, higher cash holdings, and lower capital expenditures. The results highlight banks’ rapid response to transition risks but underscore tightened credit conditions that may jeopardize polluting firms’ viability.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Loan terms tightened**: Affected firms saw loan maturities reduced by **5 months** (California) to **7 months** (Waxman-Markey) and a **25 percentage point decline** in term loan financing.  
- **Private firms disproportionately impacted**: Private firms faced **1.7 percentage point higher interest rates** and greater operational strain, while public firms’ credit terms remained largely unchanged.  
- **Shadow bank involvement**: Lenders with high GHG exposure reduced syndicated loans, increasing shadow bank (e.g., CLOs) participation by **3 percentage points** in treated firms’ loans.
