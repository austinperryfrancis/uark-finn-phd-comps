**Summary:**  
The paper by Denis and McConnell (2003) surveys international corporate governance research, dividing it into two generations. The first-generation studies, modeled after U.S. research, analyze internal mechanisms (boards, ownership) and external mechanisms (takeovers) across individual countries. Findings reveal that board composition (e.g., outsider representation) improves decision-making but not overall firm performance. Ownership structures are more concentrated outside the U.S., with blockholders (e.g., families, institutions) often enhancing value but sometimes extracting private benefits via pyramids or cross-holdings. Privatization studies show state-owned firms underperform private ones, with foreign ownership and concentrated equity linked to post-privatization gains. External takeover markets are less impactful outside the U.S. due to ownership concentration.  

The second-generation research emphasizes legal systems’ role. Strong investor protection (common law countries) correlates with dispersed ownership, developed markets, and higher firm valuations (e.g., Tobin’s Q). Weak protection (civil law countries) leads to concentrated ownership and “tunneling” by controlling shareholders. Legal frameworks influence economic growth, capital access, and governance practices. Convergence trends are debated: some countries adopt market-oriented reforms (e.g., U.K. Codes of Best Practice), but political and ownership barriers hinder systemic legal changes. Functional convergence (e.g., cross-listings) emerges as an alternative.  

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Legal Systems Dictate Governance Efficacy**: Countries with strong investor protection (common law, median ownership concentration: 45% vs. 20% in the U.S.) exhibit higher market valuations and dispersed ownership. Weak protection correlates with concentrated control and private benefits (e.g., 82% voting premium in Italy).  
- **Ownership Concentration Matters**: Blockholders improve performance in weak legal regimes but may extract private benefits. Privatized firms with foreign or concentrated equity show 10–50% higher profitability post-transition.  
- **Convergence is Partial**: While some countries adopt Anglo-Saxon practices (e.g., U.K. CEO turnover sensitivity increased post-Cadbury Code), legal/political resistance persists. Cross-listings and M&As drive functional, not systemic, convergence.
