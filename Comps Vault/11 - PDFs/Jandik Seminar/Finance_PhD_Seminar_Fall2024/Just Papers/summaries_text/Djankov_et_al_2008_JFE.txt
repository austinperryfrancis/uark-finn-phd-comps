**Summary:**  
The study introduces the "anti-self-dealing index," a novel measure evaluating legal protections for minority shareholders against expropriation by corporate insiders. Using data from 72 countries, the index focuses on private enforcement mechanisms—such as mandatory disclosure, approval by disinterested shareholders, and litigation ease—governing conflicted transactions. Results show that common law countries (average index: 0.66) have significantly stronger protections than civil law countries (average: 0.35), particularly French civil law nations. The anti-self-dealing index strongly predicts financial outcomes: a two-standard-deviation increase correlates with a 32–34 percentage point rise in stock market capitalization/GDP, a 9–10 percentage point reduction in control premiums (a proxy for private benefits of control), and a nine percentage point decline in ownership concentration. Public enforcement (fines, prison terms) had negligible effects, while private enforcement mechanisms drove market development. The index outperformed the revised anti-director rights index, underscoring its theoretical grounding in addressing self-dealing.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Anti-Self-Dealing Index Efficacy**: A two-standard-deviation increase in the index corresponds to a **32–34 percentage point rise in stock market capitalization/GDP** and a **9–10 percentage point drop in control premiums**, highlighting its predictive power for financial health.  
- **Legal Origin Impact**: Common law countries scored **0.66** on the anti-self-dealing index vs. **0.35** for civil law countries, reflecting stronger minority shareholder protections in common law systems.  
- **Private vs. Public Enforcement**: Private mechanisms (disclosure, shareholder approval, litigation) were critical to market development, while public enforcement (fines, criminal penalties) showed **no significant correlation** with positive financial outcomes.
