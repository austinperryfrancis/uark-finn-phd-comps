**Summary:**  
This study examines how Italian firms affiliated with business groups weathered the 2008 financial crisis and subsequent euro crisis more effectively than standalone firms. By leveraging granular data on firm-bank relationships and intragroup financial flows, the authors demonstrate that group affiliation enhances survival rates during banking distress. Internal capital markets allowed cash-rich firms within groups to transfer resources to cash-poor affiliates, mitigating reliance on external funding. Notably, this reallocation intensified as banks’ loan losses escalated, with funds often directed toward firms exhibiting higher sales growth, suggesting efficient resource use. Survival probabilities for group-affiliated firms were 6 percentage points higher over 2006–2013, and their failure risk was half as sensitive to bank health shocks compared to unaffiliated peers.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Survival Advantage**: Group-affiliated firms had a 6% higher survival probability (2006–2013) and faced a 0.23 pp increase in failure risk per 1 SD rise in bank loan losses, versus 0.44 pp for unaffiliated firms.  
- **Substitution Effect**: Internal capital transfers surged during banking distress (e.g., post-2011 euro crisis), with a 1 SD increase in bank loan losses amplifying cash flow-driven transfers by 53% (coefficients: -0.13 to -0.20).  
- **Efficient Allocation**: Transfers prioritized cash-constrained firms with stronger sales growth, evidenced by incremental effects (e.g., -0.07 additional transfer sensitivity for high-growth firms), aligning capital with viable opportunities.
