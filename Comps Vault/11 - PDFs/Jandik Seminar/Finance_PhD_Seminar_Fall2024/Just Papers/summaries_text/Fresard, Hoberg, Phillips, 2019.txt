**Summary**  
This study examines how the stage of innovation (unrealized vs. realized) influences vertical acquisitions. Using product descriptions from firms’ 10-K filings and vertical linkages from input-output tables, the authors construct a dynamic network of vertical relatedness between firms. They find that R&D-intensive firms (unrealized innovation) are **less likely to be acquired by vertical buyers**, as maintaining independence preserves incentives for ongoing innovation. Conversely, firms with patented innovations (realized innovation) are **more likely to be acquired vertically**, as patents mitigate holdup risks and align commercialization incentives.  

The results hold across robustness checks, including controls for reverse causality and alternative measures of innovation efficiency. For example, a 1-standard-deviation increase in R&D intensity reduces the probability of vertical acquisition by **0.46 percentage points** (≈33% of the baseline rate), while a similar increase in patenting intensity raises it by **0.56 percentage points**. These effects are amplified in contexts where incentive provision is challenging (e.g., high inventor mobility) or holdup risks are acute (e.g., patent litigation exposure). Case studies, such as Western Digital’s acquisition of Komag, illustrate how vertical integration optimizes control over realized innovation.  

**Key Takeaways**  
1. **R&D deters vertical acquisitions**: Higher R&D intensity (unrealized innovation) reduces acquisition likelihood (coefficient: **−0.092***), as preserving independence maintains innovation incentives.  
2. **Patents facilitate vertical deals**: Patenting intensity (realized innovation) increases acquisition probability (coefficient: **+0.112***), as legal protection reduces holdup risks.  
3. **Context matters**:  
   - R&D’s deterrent effect strengthens when retaining post-acquisition talent is difficult (e.g., high inventor mobility).  
   - Patents’ positive effect is stronger in industries with holdup risks (e.g., low patent liquidity, high litigation).
