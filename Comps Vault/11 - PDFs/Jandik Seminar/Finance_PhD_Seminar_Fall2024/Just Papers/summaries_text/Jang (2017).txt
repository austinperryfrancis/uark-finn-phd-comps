**Summary:**  
The study investigates how U.S. multinational corporations (MNCs) leverage international diversification to enhance financial flexibility, particularly during the 2007–2009 financial crisis. Using data on bank loans, bond issuances, and subsidiary locations, the research finds that MNCs are significantly more likely than domestic firms to access foreign funding sources, such as foreign bank loans (13.3 percentage points higher probability) and international bond markets (5.2 percentage points higher likelihood). During the crisis, MNCs shifted borrowing to foreign lenders in less-affected regions where they had operational subsidiaries, mitigating domestic credit constraints. This financial flexibility allowed MNCs to reduce U.S. investment cuts by 1.8–2.1 percentage points less than domestic firms, as they relied on foreign capital to sustain domestic operations. The findings underscore the role of geographic diversification in buffering firms against localized financial shocks.

**Key Takeaways:**  
1. **Foreign Funding Access**: MNCs had a 13.3% higher likelihood of foreign lender participation in loans and a 5.2% greater propensity to issue international bonds compared to domestic firms, reflecting enhanced access to global capital markets.  
2. **Crisis Resilience**: During the 2007–2009 crisis, MNCs increased foreign borrowing by 23.7 percentage points post-Lehman collapse, particularly from countries with operational subsidiaries, reducing reliance on strained U.S. credit markets.  
3. **Investment Stability**: MNCs curtailed U.S. capital expenditures 1.8–2.1 percentage points less than domestic firms during the crisis, demonstrating how international diversification preserves investment during financial shocks.
