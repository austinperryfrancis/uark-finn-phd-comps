**Summary:**  
This study introduces a novel method to estimate Tobin’s Q for conglomerate divisions without relying on standalone firms, addressing limitations of traditional approaches that assume divisional and standalone Qs are interchangeable. By analyzing conglomerates as portfolios of divisions and applying median regressions, the authors derive divisional Qs that systematically differ from standalone Qs across industries and over time. Key findings reveal conglomerate divisions exhibit 17% lower Q volatility and 54% reduced sensitivity to credit shocks compared to standalone firms, attributed to internal capital markets mitigating external financing frictions. For instance, divisional Qs in energy and healthcare sectors trade at significant discounts (averaging -56% and -31%, respectively), while consumer nondurables and telecommunications divisions trade at premiums. The study further demonstrates that internal investment decisions align more closely with divisional Qs (e.g., 2.3% sensitivity) than standalone proxies, and differences in Qs predict diversifying acquisition activity and announcement returns.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Divisional vs. Standalone Q Differences**: Conglomerate divisions’ Qs vary widely from standalone firms, with relative divisional Qs (RDQs) ranging from -56% (energy) to +16% (telecoms) across industries.  
- **Credit Shock Insulation**: Divisional Q sensitivity to credit shocks (e.g., TED spreads, default risk) is 54% lower than standalone firms, driven by internal capital reallocation and low covariance among divisions.  
- **Real Investment Effects**: Conglomerate investment is more responsive to divisional Qs (coefficient 0.023 post-spinoff vs. 0.015 for standalone Qs), and RDQs predict diversifying acquisition volume (+8.5% per 0.22 RDQ increase) and returns (+0.8% announcement returns per RDQ standard deviation).
