**Summary**  
The study examines how Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) influences corporate shareholder engagement using a quasi-natural experiment around the 70% threshold for say-on-pay (SOP) votes. Firms falling below this threshold face ISS scrutiny, prompting them to enhance engagement to avoid negative voting recommendations. Results show ISS-treated firms (with SOP votes just below 70%) exhibit significant increases in both the likelihood (31% higher probability) and intensity of engagement, particularly when boards face agency conflicts or directors risk losing ISS support. These engagement efforts persist over time, suggesting long-term changes in governance practices. Shareholders value this ISS-driven engagement, as evidenced by 2–3% abnormal stock returns around SOP disclosures for treated firms that received prior ISS "against" recommendations. The findings highlight ISS's role in fostering bidirectional communication between firms and investors, countering critiques of one-size-fits-all governance influence.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **ISS significantly boosts engagement**: Firms receiving ISS scrutiny after low SOP votes (<70%) increased engagement probability by **14.9 percentage points** (RD analysis) and were **31% more likely to engage** (linear regression).  
- **Market validation**: Treated firms with prior ISS "against" recommendations saw **2–3% abnormal stock returns**, indicating shareholder approval of ISS-induced engagement.  
- **Persistence and governance role**: Engagement persisted beyond ISS scrutiny, particularly at firms with higher agency conflicts (e.g., long-tenured compensation committees), suggesting ISS substitutes for weak governance.
