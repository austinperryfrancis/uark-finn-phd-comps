**Summary**  
DeAngelo and Roll (2015) challenge the prevailing view that corporate capital structures are stable, demonstrating instead that leverage ratios exhibit significant instability over time. Analyzing data from 1950 to 2008, they find that leverage cross-sections (distributions of leverage across firms) lose similarity as the time between them increases, with near-zero correlation after 20 years. Most firms experience substantial shifts in leverage, with 69.5% of long-lived firms moving across three or more quartiles of the leverage distribution. Stable leverage regimes, when they occur, are temporary and concentrated at low levels (e.g., Debt/TA < 0.2). Industry-median leverage also fluctuates widely, underscoring the importance of time-varying factors. Simulations reveal that models with time-varying targets or slow adjustment speeds (e.g., 15% annually) better explain observed instability than fixed-target or random models, suggesting firms prioritize flexibility over rigid debt-equity ratios.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Instability of Leverage Cross-Sections**: Leverage distributions diverge significantly over time, with R² values between cross-sections dropping to near-zero after 20 years.  
- **Within-Firm Leverage Volatility**: 69.5% of firms with 20+ years of data occupy three or more leverage quartiles, and the median Debt/TA range is 0.391, reflecting pronounced variability.  
- **Empirical Support for Flexible Targeting**: Models with time-varying targets or slow adjustment speeds (e.g., 15% annual speed of adjustment) outperform fixed-target models, aligning with observed leverage dynamics.
