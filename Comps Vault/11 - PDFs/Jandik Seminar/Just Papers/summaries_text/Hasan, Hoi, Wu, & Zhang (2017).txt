**Summary**  
This study examines how social capital, defined as cooperative social norms and dense networks within U.S. counties, influences debt contracting. Analyzing 32,425 bank loans and 2,937 public bonds from 1990–2012, the authors find firms in high-social-capital counties incur lower loan spreads (4.33 basis points reduction per 1 standard deviation increase in social capital) and bond yields, with bond spreads dropping nearly twice as much as loan spreads. These results hold after controlling for religiosity, tax avoidance, and corporate social responsibility. A quasi-experiment leveraging headquarters relocations (e.g., firms moving to higher-social-capital areas saw loan spreads decrease by 12.8 basis points more than those moving to lower-social-capital counties) and instrumental variable analyses confirm causality. Banks also impose fewer collateral and covenant requirements on such firms, which increasingly prefer bond financing over bank loans, signaling lenders’ perception of reduced opportunistic behavior risk.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Loan Spread Reduction**: A 1 standard deviation increase in social capital reduces bank loan spreads by **4.33 basis points** (coefficient: **−0.025**).  
- **Stronger Impact on Bonds**: Social capital lowers public bond spreads more sharply than loans, with a coefficient of **−0.078** (vs. −0.025 for loans).  
- **Causal Evidence**: Firms relocating to high-social-capital areas experience **12.8 basis point** greater reductions in loan spreads (diff-in-diff coefficient: **−0.128**), confirming social capital’s causal role.
