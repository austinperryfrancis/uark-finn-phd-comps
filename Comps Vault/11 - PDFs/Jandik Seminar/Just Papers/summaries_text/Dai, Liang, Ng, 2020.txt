**Summary:**  
This study investigates whether socially responsible corporate customers can propagate corporate social responsibility (CSR) practices to their suppliers across global supply chains. Using data from 34,117 customer-supplier pairs across 50 countries (2003–2015), the authors find a unilateral effect: customers’ CSR ratings significantly improve suppliers’ subsequent CSR performance, but not vice versa. Robust identification strategies, including regression discontinuity designs (RDD) around close-call CSR shareholder proposals and exogenous product scandal shocks, confirm causality. A one-standard-deviation increase in a customer’s CSR rating leads to an 8% aggregate rise in suppliers’ CSR. Mechanisms include **positive assortative matching** (customers selecting/retaining CSR-aligned suppliers) and customer influence via bargaining power (e.g., in less R&D-intensive or competitive industries) and network ties (common ownership or board interlocks). Collaborative CSR efforts enhance operational efficiency and valuation for both parties, but only customers benefit from increased future sales growth.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Unilateral CSR Influence**: Customers’ CSR drives suppliers’ CSR improvements (coefficient: ~0.04 in baseline models), but not the reverse. A one-standard-deviation increase in customer CSR boosts supplier CSR by 8% through direct networks.  
- **Causal Evidence**: Close-call CSR proposal passage (RDD) raises suppliers’ CSR scores by 24% of a standard deviation. Product scandals amplify customer pressure, increasing suppliers’ product responsibility ratings by 9–11%.  
- **Economic Impact**: Collaborative CSR improves operational efficiency (lower SG&A expenses) and valuation for both firms, but only customers see significant sales growth. Bargaining power (via low supplier R&D/patents or high industry concentration) and network ties (common investors/directors) strengthen CSR propagation.
