**Summary:**  
The research paper investigates the significant rise in U.S. corporate cash holdings, revealing that the increase is predominantly concentrated in the foreign subsidiaries of multinational corporations (MNCs). While precautionary savings motives (e.g., liquidity needs, investment uncertainty) explain domestic cash holdings, foreign cash accumulation is driven by tax optimization strategies. Declining foreign tax rates relative to U.S. rates, coupled with relaxed regulations like "check-the-box," enabled firms—especially those with intellectual property (e.g., R&D-intensive firms)—to shift income to low-tax jurisdictions. This income shifting, facilitated by intercompany transactions ("related sales"), led to trapped foreign cash. The study finds that 79% of the rise in foreign cash-to-assets ratios (4.1% of the 5.2% total increase) is attributable to falling foreign tax rates, with 92% of foreign cash growth occurring in firms combining R&D and related sales.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Tax Rates Drive Foreign Cash:** A 1% drop in the effective foreign tax rate increased foreign cash holdings by 0.74 percentage points (coefficient: -0.743). Declining foreign tax rates explained 79% of the rise in foreign cash.  
- **Role of Intangible Assets:** Firms with R&D expenditures and intra-firm sales ("related sales") accumulated 92% of foreign cash growth, as intellectual property enabled income shifting to low-tax jurisdictions.  
- **Domestic vs. Foreign Cash Motives:** Domestic cash aligns with precautionary savings (e.g., R&D, leverage, volatility), while foreign cash is tax-driven, showing no link to precautionary factors.
