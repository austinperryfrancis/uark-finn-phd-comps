**Summary:**  
This study investigates how international trade networks facilitate the spread of merger waves across countries from 1989 to 2016. Using global trade data and merger activity records, the authors construct trade-weighted measures of connected countries’ merger activity. They find that a country’s likelihood of experiencing a merger wave (cross-border or domestic) is significantly influenced by merger activity in its trade partners, even after controlling for domestic lagged merger activity and economic factors. For example, a one-unit increase in trade-weighted merger activity (e.g., "Subject Imports from Connected" coefficient: 16.55***) raises the probability of a high merger state by ~30%. Instrumental variable analyses using tariff cuts and sanctions confirm causality, while placebo tests and controls for economic integration (e.g., cultural distance, stock correlations) reinforce the robustness of trade as a propagation channel.  

The study also highlights the role of learning and exposure: firms engaged in trade gain knowledge and incentives to undertake cross-border mergers. Cultural differences (e.g., Hofstede dimensions) and weak property rights in target countries reduce this effect, whereas institutional quality enhances it. Granger causality tests validate that trade flows precede mergers, not vice versa. The densification of global trade networks over time underscores the growing interconnectedness of merger activity worldwide.  

**Key Takeaways:**  
1. **Trade-Weighted Merger Activity Drives Waves**: A country’s merger activity is strongly predicted by its trade partners’ merger activity (e.g., coefficients of 16.55*** for "Subject Imports from Connected" and 17.29*** for "Subject Exports to Connected").  
2. **Causal Role of Trade Confirmed**: Instrumental variable models (tariff cuts/sanctions) show trade linkages causally propagate mergers (2SLS coefficients ~9–10***).  
3. **Cultural and Institutional Boundaries Matter**: Cultural distance reduces merger spillovers (negative coefficients), while strong property rights (e.g., ICRG investment profile interaction: 3.54***) amplify the effect, highlighting learning and risk mitigation mechanisms.
