**Summary**  
The study examines how debt misvaluation influences merger and acquisition (M&A) activity, particularly the shifting dominance between financial buyers (private equity firms) and strategic buyers (operating companies). The authors propose that debt market conditions interact with structural differences in governance and project diversification. Financial buyers, leveraging superior monitoring and stand-alone project evaluations, exploit debt overvaluation more effectively than strategic buyers, who combine projects (creating coinsurance effects that reduce perceived risk). Empirical analysis using Moody’s Average Position (AP)—a measure of debt misvaluation based on ex-post rating accuracy—reveals that overvalued debt correlates with increased PE activity. For instance, a 1% decrease in aggregate AP (indicating overvaluation) raises PE activity by 3.2%, with stronger effects for conglomerate strategic acquirers due to reduced coinsurance benefits.  

**Key Takeaways**  
1. **Debt misvaluation drives PE dominance**: Overvalued debt markets (lower AP) significantly increase PE activity relative to strategic buyers. A 1% decline in aggregate AP boosts PE’s share of M&A value by 3.2% (coefficient: -3.23***).  
2. **Coinsurance disadvantages strategics**: Strategic buyers, especially conglomerates, are less able to exploit debt overvaluation. PE’s share relative to conglomerates rises by 4.83% per 1% AP drop (coefficient: -4.83***).  
3. **Moral hazard and leverage**: Overvaluation amplifies PE’s governance advantage, enabling higher leverage. High debt issuance periods (HIGH_1D) intensify this effect, with interaction terms showing significant negative coefficients (e.g., -5.42*).
