**Summary:**  
This study examines how SEC Rule 10b-18's price ceilings on share repurchases and evolving market structures influenced the shift from dividends to repurchases. The authors find that reforms in the 1990s–2000s (e.g., the 1994 Manning Rule, 1997 Order Handling Rules, tick-size reductions, and automated trading) reduced frictions, enabling firms to compete more effectively under price constraints, leading to a secular rise in repurchases. Conversely, the 2016 Tick Size Pilot Program, which reversed some reforms by widening tick sizes and restricting dark pool trading, reduced repurchases by 21% for treated firms. These frictions explain 18% of the long-term increase in repurchases and why dividends persist. Mechanisms include "queuing competition" (issuers losing priority to high-frequency traders) and reduced dark pool access, particularly under the Pilot’s Trade-at Rule.  

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Price ceilings and market structure reforms drive repurchases:** The Manning Rule (60% repurchase increase), 1997 tick-size reduction (41% increase), and NYSE autoquotes (25% increase) eased competition at price ceilings, while the 2016 Tick Size Pilot reversed gains (21% reduction).  
- **Queuing and dark pool channels matter:** Tick-constrained firms saw 45% fewer repurchases under the Pilot due to heightened queue competition; dark pool restrictions (Test Group 3) amplified declines (55% vs. 36% in other groups).  
- **18% of secular increase explained:** Market-structure reforms account for 18% of the rise in repurchases from 1995–2021, leaving residual frictions that prevent dividends from being fully displaced.
