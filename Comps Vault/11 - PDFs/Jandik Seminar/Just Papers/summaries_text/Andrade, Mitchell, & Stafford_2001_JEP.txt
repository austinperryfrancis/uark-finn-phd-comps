**Summary:**  
The paper examines mergers and acquisitions (M&A) from the 1970s to the 1990s, highlighting that mergers create shareholder value, with most gains accruing to target firms (16% average three-day abnormal return). Mergers cluster by industry and occur in waves, driven by external shocks like deregulation, which accounted for nearly half of 1990s activity. The 1990s saw a shift toward friendly, stock-financed deals within related industries. While combined acquirer-target returns are positive (1.8% on average), acquirers alone see negligible or negative returns, particularly in stock-financed deals. Long-term post-merger performance improvements are modest (1% relative to industry peers), but methodological challenges complicate conclusions on sustained value creation.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Deregulation was pivotal**: Nearly 50% of 1990s M&A activity stemmed from deregulation in industries like banking, telecom, and utilities.  
- **Targets dominate gains**: Target shareholders earned a median 16% three-day abnormal return, while acquirers averaged -0.7% (significant -1.5% for stock-financed deals).  
- **Payment method matters**: Combined returns for cash-only deals were 3.6%, but stock-financed mergers showed no net value creation (0% abnormal returns).
