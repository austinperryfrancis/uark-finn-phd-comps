**Summary:**  
This study investigates how incumbent airlines adjust their debt structure in anticipation of competition from low-cost carriers. Using data from the U.S. airline industry (1990–2014), the author identifies threatened routes based on low-cost airlines’ expansion patterns, where entry at one airport endpoint significantly raises the likelihood of entering the full route. Threatened incumbents increase their debt maturity (shifting to long-term debt) to mitigate rollover risk—the danger of being unable to refinance debt during financial stress caused by competition. This response is pronounced among financially constrained and speculative-grade firms. However, leverage ratios remain unchanged, indicating airlines prioritize maturity structure over debt levels when facing competitive threats.  

**Key Takeaways:**  
1. **Debt Maturity Adjustment**: A one standard deviation increase in threat exposure leads to a **6.0 percentage point rise** in the proportion of long-term debt (10% increase from a 60% baseline), reducing rollover risk.  
2. **Entry Threat Impact**: Low-cost carrier entry at one route endpoint raises the probability of full route entry by **25 percentage points within a year** and **36 percentage points within three years**, driving incumbents’ preemptive financial adjustments.  
3. **Heterogeneous Effects**: Firms with speculative-grade debt ratings or financial constraints exhibit stronger responses, with constrained airlines increasing debt maturity by **16 percentage points** (vs. 6 points for the average firm). Leverage remains unaffected, highlighting the focus on debt structure over debt volume.
