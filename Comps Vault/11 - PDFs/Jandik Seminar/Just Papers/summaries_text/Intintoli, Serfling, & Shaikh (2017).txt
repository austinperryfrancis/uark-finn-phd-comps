**Summary**  
The study examines how CEO turnovers at customer firms disrupt relationships with dependent suppliers, defined as those relying on a customer for ≥10% of revenue. Analyzing data from 1993–2011, the authors find that suppliers lose an average of $19.1 million in sales to a customer post-CEO turnover, with a 12% increased risk of the relationship ending. Supplier stock returns also decline significantly (−0.61% to −2.73%) around CEO departure announcements. Losses are more severe when the outgoing CEO was entrenched (e.g., high decision-making power, weak board oversight) or replaced by an outsider, and when suppliers had made relationship-specific investments (RSIs). Conversely, higher customer switching costs mitigate disruptions. The primary mechanism is asset divestiture by new CEOs, not price renegotiations. Results persist even for exogenous turnovers (e.g., retirements, deaths), supporting a causal interpretation.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **CEO turnovers reduce supplier sales**: Post-turnover, suppliers experience an 11.9% decline in customer-specific sales (coefficient −0.127) and a 2.6% drop in total sales, equivalent to $19.1 million per supplier.  
- **Impact varies by CEO and governance**: Losses are 27.9% larger with outsider CEOs and 30.5% larger in forced turnovers. Entrenched CEOs (e.g., power index 1 SD higher) reduce supplier CARs by 0.87–0.95%.  
- **Mechanism and moderators**: Declines stem from asset divestment, not pricing changes. Relationship-specific investments (RSIs) worsen losses, while customer switching costs (e.g., longer relationships, supplier market share) buffer them.
