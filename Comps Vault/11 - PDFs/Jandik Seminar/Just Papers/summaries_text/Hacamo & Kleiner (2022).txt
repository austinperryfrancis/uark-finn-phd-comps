**Summary**  
This study examines how social networks influence firms' ability to recruit talented managers, leveraging a natural experiment where MBA students at Indiana University were randomly assigned to teams and cohorts. Using a discrete-choice model and data from LinkedIn, GMAT scores, and surveys, the authors find that social connections to former employees significantly increase the likelihood of hiring high-ability managers (measured by cognitive skills like quantitative GMAT scores), with no effect on low-ability hires. Effects are strongest for nonlocal firms, peers in the same team or neighborhood (strong ties), and firms with less public visibility. Survey evidence indicates networks facilitate recruitment by reducing informational asymmetries, as connected students were 19% more likely to apply to a firm. Notably, 40% of graduates joined top- or mid-tier firms post-MBA, with connected hires showing 2–3 point higher GMAT scores than peers at the same firm.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Networks Boost High-Ability Hiring**: Social connections (e.g., through team members) increase the probability of hiring high-ability managers by 3.5–4.8 percentage points for top- and mid-tier firms (odds ratios: 2.22 for top-tier, 2.99 for mid-tier). Low-ability hiring remains unaffected.  
- **Strength of Ties Matters**: Team connections (strong ties) had 10x larger per-individual effects than cohort connections (weak ties), though aggregated cohort effects were comparable. Geographically proximate or same-gender peers amplified network impacts.  
- **Information Channel**: Firms connected to high-ability teams recruited managers with 2–3 point higher GMAT scores. Surveys revealed a 19% increase in job applications to connected firms, driven by information sharing about firm fundamentals.
