**Summary:**  
This study examines how personal connections between executives influence supplier selection in supply chains, addressing information asymmetry. Analyzing data from U.S. public firms (2000–2011), the authors find vendors with educational or prior employment connections to a customer’s executives are 60% more likely to be selected as suppliers compared to unconnected peers (coefficient: 0.217, 21% absolute increase over a 35% baseline). The effect is stronger for C-suite executives (e.g., COOs) and in high-information-asymmetry contexts, such as geographically distant firms or vendors with poor accounting quality. Connections also reduce contract restrictiveness (coefficient: -0.735) and extend contract duration (coefficient: +0.471 years). When connected executives depart, supply-chain relationships terminate 2.64 times faster, highlighting the role of personal ties in sustaining partnerships. Post-formation, customers experience improved operational efficiency, including lower inventory volatility and higher ROA (interaction coefficient: +0.007).  

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Personal connections significantly boost supplier selection likelihood**, increasing the probability by 60% (coefficient: 0.217).  
- **Contracts with connected suppliers are less restrictive and longer**, reducing restrictiveness by 0.735 provisions and extending duration by ~6 months.  
- **Executive departures disrupt connected relationships**, with a 2.64× higher termination hazard, while connected partnerships enhance customer performance (e.g., +0.007 ROA post-formation).
