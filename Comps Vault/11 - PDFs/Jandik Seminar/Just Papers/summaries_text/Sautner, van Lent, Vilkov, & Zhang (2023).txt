**Summary:**  
The study introduces a novel method to measure firm-level exposure to climate change by analyzing earnings call transcripts using a machine learning algorithm. The approach identifies climate-related discussions tied to **opportunities** (e.g., renewable energy), **physical risks** (e.g., extreme weather), and **regulatory shocks** (e.g., carbon taxes). The dataset spans 10,000+ firms across 34 countries (2002–2020) and reveals that firms with higher climate exposure scores are more likely to create green jobs, file green patents, and exhibit priced risks in financial markets. The measures capture nuanced, forward-looking insights from analyst-manager dialogues, validated through audits and correlations with emissions and public climate attention.  

Key findings highlight significant **within-industry variation**, with 70–96% of exposure differences driven by firm-level factors (e.g., management strategies) rather than sector or country trends. High-exposure firms drive the net-zero transition: a one-standard-deviation increase in climate exposure predicts a **109% rise in green-tech jobs** and a **72% increase in green patents**. Conversely, these firms reduce nongreen hiring and innovation. In financial markets, climate exposure correlates with options pricing (e.g., **4.5% higher tail-risk costs**) and equity risk premiums, with a climate factor showing an average **3.7% annualized risk premium**.  

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Green Transition Drivers**: A one-standard-deviation increase in climate exposure predicts a **109% surge in green job creation** and **72% more green patents**, signaling firms’ strategic shifts toward sustainability.  
- **Firm-Level Dominance**: 70–96% of climate exposure variation stems from firm-specific factors (e.g., innovation capacity), not industry or geography, underscoring the role of idiosyncratic strategies.  
- **Financial Market Pricing**: Climate risks are priced in options (e.g., **4.5% higher costs for downside protection**) and equities, with a climate factor commanding a **3.7% annual risk premium**, reflecting investor demand for hedging climate uncertainty.
