**Summary**  
This research paper investigates corporate donations to charities affiliated with independent directors ("affiliated donations") and their impact on governance. Such donations, often undisclosed, create conflicts of interest that weaken directors' monitoring incentives. Firms making affiliated donations pay CEOs 9.4% more on average than non-donating firms, with compensation rising significantly (15.6% higher) when donations involve the compensation committee chair or a majority of its members. The study also finds that CEOs at donating firms are less likely to be replaced for poor performance, particularly when donations are large or target a majority of directors. Instrumental variable analysis (using exogenous shocks like college coach turnovers) supports a causal link between donations and higher CEO pay. Poorer stock returns, lower Tobin’s Q, and reduced CEO turnover sensitivity to performance further suggest governance erosion. The authors argue for mandatory disclosure of these donations to improve transparency.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Higher CEO Compensation**: CEO pay is **9.4% higher** at firms with affiliated donations, rising to **15.6%** when donations involve the compensation committee chair.  
- **Reduced Accountability**: CEOs are less likely to be replaced for poor performance when donations target a large fraction of the board or are substantial in size.  
- **Governance Costs**: Affiliated donations correlate with lower firm valuation (**Tobin’s Q reduced by 0.24**) and weaker stock performance (**2.4 percentage points lower returns**), highlighting agency costs.
