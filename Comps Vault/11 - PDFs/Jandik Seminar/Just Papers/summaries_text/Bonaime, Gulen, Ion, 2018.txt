**Summary:**  
This study examines how policy uncertainty, measured by the Baker et al. (2016) index (BBD), impacts mergers and acquisitions (M&A). At the macro level, a one standard deviation increase in policy uncertainty reduces aggregate M&A deal value by 6.6% and the number of deals by 3.9% over 12 months, with no subsequent recovery, indicating lost—not delayed—deals. Firm-level analysis shows an 11.74% decline in acquisition likelihood (from 14% to 12.35%) under high policy uncertainty. The effect is amplified for irreversible investments and firms sensitive to policy shifts (e.g., those reliant on government spending) but mitigated for competitive or risk-hedging deals (e.g., cross-border or vertical mergers). The dominant mechanism is the real options channel: uncertainty incentivizes delaying investments. Additionally, policy uncertainty strengthens targets’ bargaining power, increasing deal premiums and reducing acquirer termination fees.  

The study identifies monetary/fiscal policies, taxes, government spending, and regulation as key uncertainty drivers, whereas healthcare or trade policies have minimal impact. These findings underscore how specific policy ambiguities hinder capital reallocation through M&A, with implications for regulators and firms.  

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **Policy Uncertainty Reduces M&A Activity**: A 1 SD increase in the BBD index decreases aggregate deal value by **6.6%** and deal count by **3.9%** within a year, while firm-level acquisition likelihood drops by **11.74%**.  
- **Real Options Drive Decision-Making**: The negative effect is strongest for irreversible deals (e.g., capital-intensive industries) and firms with high policy exposure (e.g., government-dependent sectors), highlighting the value of delaying investments amid uncertainty.  
- **Policy Type Matters**: Uncertainty in taxes, fiscal/monetary policy, and regulation has the largest impact, whereas health or trade policies show negligible effects. Targets gain leverage in negotiations, evidenced by higher premiums and fewer termination fees.
