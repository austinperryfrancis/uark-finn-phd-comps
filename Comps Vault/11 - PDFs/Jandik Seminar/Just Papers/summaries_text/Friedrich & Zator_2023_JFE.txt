**Summary**  
The study examines how Danish exporters responded to the 2006 boycott triggered by the publication of Muhammad caricatures, which caused a drastic decline in exports to Muslim countries. Using firm-level data, the authors find that while affected firms lost 56–78% of exports to Muslim markets (equivalent to a ~6% total sales decline on average), low-leverage firms (with liabilities/assets below the median) fully offset losses by redirecting sales to non-Muslim markets. These firms expanded into new product-destination markets, increasing non-Muslim exports by ~16% and maintaining total sales. In contrast, high-leverage firms failed to expand, experiencing a 6.6% sales decline and employment reductions. The divergence is attributed to financial constraints: low-leverage firms increased investment and debt to fund market entry, while high-leverage firms downsized and outsourced labor to cut costs.  

**Key Takeaways**  
1. **Financial leverage critically shapes crisis resilience**: Low-leverage firms offset boycott-induced losses by entering new markets (16% increase in non-Muslim exports), while high-leverage firms saw a 6.6% sales decline (*β = -0.2328*** for high-leverage firms’ export response).  
2. **Market entry vs. downsizing**: Low-leverage firms expanded product-country pairs (+18.96% new markets, *p < 0.01*), whereas high-leverage firms reduced employment (-5.4%, *p < 0.05*) and increased outsourcing.  
3. **Financial flexibility enables adaptation**: Low-leverage firms raised debt and SG&A spending (+6.4%, *p < 0.01*) to fund expansion, while high-leverage firms faced constraints, cutting labor costs and halting investment (*β = -0.2739*** for investment response).
