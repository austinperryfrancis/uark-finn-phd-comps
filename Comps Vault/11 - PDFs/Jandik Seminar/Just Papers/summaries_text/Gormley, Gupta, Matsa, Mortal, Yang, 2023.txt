**Summary**  
The study examines the impact of campaigns by the "Big Three" institutional investors (State Street, BlackRock, Vanguard) launched in 2017 to increase gender diversity on corporate boards. Using a difference-in-differences approach, the authors find that firms with higher pre-campaign Big Three ownership significantly increased female board representation from 2016–2019. These firms added 2.5 times as many female directors by 2019, driven by expanding recruitment beyond traditional networks (e.g., 67% fewer CEO-connected candidates) and reducing emphasis on prior CEO experience (14% decline). The rise was not symbolic; female directors gained influential roles (e.g., committee chairs), and the campaigns accounted for 33–67% of the overall diversity increase. The results underscore institutional investors’ ability to drive governance changes and the importance of investor pressure over mandates in fostering meaningful diversity.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Substantial Impact**: A one standard deviation increase in Big Three ownership correlated with a **76% rise** in female director appointments (coefficient: 1.133) and an **11% increase** in female board share (coefficient: 0.180).  
- **Recruitment Shifts**: Firms reduced reliance on CEO experience (14% decline) and existing networks (67% fewer connections to CEOs among new female hires).  
- **Beyond Tokenism**: Female directors were more likely to hold key leadership roles, such as committee chairs, indicating deeper integration into governance (significant coefficients for committee roles in director-level analysis).
