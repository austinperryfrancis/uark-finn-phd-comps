**Summary**  
The study investigates whether asymmetric information, as posited by the pecking order theory, drives corporate capital structure decisions. Using a novel adverse selection index derived from market microstructure measures (e.g., bid-ask spreads, probability of informed trading, and liquidity proxies), the authors analyze U.S. firms from 1973–2002. They find that higher asymmetric information leads firms to rely more heavily on debt financing. Firms in the highest adverse selection decile issued 30 cents more debt per dollar of financing deficit compared to the lowest decile. Results persist after controlling for traditional determinants (size, tangibility, profitability), stock volatility, turnover, and insider trading, underscoring that the pecking order theory holds when information asymmetry is significant.  

The findings suggest that asymmetric information is a key conditional driver of capital structure, explaining the mixed success of the pecking order in prior studies. The index’s robustness highlights the value of market-based measures over static firm characteristics. The study reconciles conflicting evidence by showing the theory’s applicability depends on the severity of adverse selection costs, aligning with Myers’ (1984) conditional framework.  

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Debt reliance increases with asymmetric information**: Firms in the highest adverse selection decile issued **30% more debt** per dollar of financing deficit than those in the lowest decile.  
- **Novel index validates pecking order conditionally**: The adverse selection index (combining microstructure proxies like PIN and bid-ask spreads) robustly predicts financing choices, even after controlling for firm characteristics and liquidity factors.  
- **Coefficients highlight incremental sensitivity**: A one-unit increase in the adverse selection index led to a **0.021 rise in the debt-deficit coefficient** (γ = 0.021, *t*-stat = 16.0), indicating heightened debt issuance for firms facing greater information asymmetry.
