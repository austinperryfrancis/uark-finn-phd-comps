**Summary**  
The study investigates how multinational firms transmit financial shocks across borders through internal capital markets. Analyzing a credit supply shock to German parent firms from Commerzbank’s 2008/09 lending cut, the authors show that international affiliates supported their parents by increasing internal lending, which constrained their own finances and led to significant real economic declines (e.g., sales dropped by 9.7 log points on average from 2008–2010). These effects were more severe for affiliates with preexisting internal lending ties and those in countries with less developed credit markets. Managers exhibited "Darwinist" behavior internationally, favoring high-growth affiliates, but were "Socialist" domestically, shielding German affiliates regardless of performance. The transmission of financial shocks via internal capital markets had large aggregate impacts, reducing foreign economies’ sales by up to 0.4% (e.g., Austria, Czech Republic), while nonfinancial shocks (e.g., floods) showed weaker transmission due to parents’ access to external financing.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **Internal capital markets amplify financial crises**: Affiliates with higher parent Commerzbank dependence saw sales drop by **9.7 log points** (2008–2010) as they lent internally to constrained parents, reducing their own growth.  
- **Managerial bias**: Firms prioritized "Darwinist" efficiency for international affiliates (weaker affiliates suffered more) but displayed "Socialist" protectionism domestically, insulating German affiliates from shocks.  
- **Credit market development mitigates spillovers**: Affiliates in countries with higher credit/GDP ratios experienced **smaller real effects**, though even developed markets saw significant declines. Financial shocks propagated more strongly than nonfinancial ones (e.g., floods), underscoring the role of external financing access.
