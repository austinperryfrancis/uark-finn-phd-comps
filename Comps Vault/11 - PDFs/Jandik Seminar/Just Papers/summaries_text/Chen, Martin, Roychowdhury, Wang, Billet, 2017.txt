**Summary**  
The study investigates how internal information asymmetry (IIA) between divisional and top managers in conglomerates affects external communication quality. The authors develop DIFRET, a measure of IIA based on the difference in trading profits from informed insider trades between divisional and corporate managers. Validated through tests linking DIFRET to divisional volatility, geographic/logistical barriers, and CEO turnover, the study finds that higher IIA (reflected in elevated DIFRET) leads to less accurate, more pessimistic (-0.042 coefficient), and less specific (-0.102 coefficient) management forecasts, as well as reduced forecast frequency. Firms with higher DIFRET are also more likely to issue error-driven financial restatements (2.65 percentage-point increase per standard deviation), though not fraud-related ones. Instrumental variable analyses and robustness checks confirm that constraints on internal information flow impair external disclosure quality.

**Key Takeaways**  
- **IIA undermines forecast quality**: A one-standard-deviation increase in DIFRET reduces management forecast accuracy by 0.33 percentage points (coefficient: -0.018, p<0.01) and increases pessimistic bias by 0.77 points (coefficient: -0.042, p<0.05).  
- **Error restatements rise with IIA**: Higher DIFRET correlates with a 2.65 percentage-point increase in error-driven restatements (coefficient: 0.638, p<0.05).  
- **Contextual drivers matter**: Reduced flight time between divisions/headquarters and weaker non-compete enforcement lower IIA, validating DIFRET. Findings highlight the role of internal information flow in external transparency.
