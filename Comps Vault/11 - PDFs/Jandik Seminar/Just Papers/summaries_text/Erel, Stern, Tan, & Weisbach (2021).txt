**Summary:**  
The research paper investigates whether machine learning (ML) algorithms can enhance corporate director selection by predicting director performance. Using data from U.S. public firms (2000–2014), the authors trained ML models (e.g., XGBoost, Lasso) to forecast director effectiveness, measured by shareholder voting support in reelections, dissent rates (<90% support), and early turnover. ML algorithms outperformed traditional OLS models in out-of-sample accuracy, with directors predicted to perform poorly by ML receiving significantly lower shareholder support (e.g., bottom decile directors ranked at the 23rd percentile vs. top decile at 80th percentile relative to alternatives). Predictably underperforming directors were more likely to be male, hold multiple directorships, and have extensive networks. Firms with weak governance (e.g., high E-index, co-opted boards) were more prone to selecting these directors, suggesting agency conflicts influence nominations.

**Key Takeaways:**  
- **ML Predicts Director Performance Effectively**: ML models (e.g., XGBoost) accurately forecasted director outcomes, with a 57-percentile gap in shareholder support between the top and bottom deciles of predicted performance.  
- **Overvalued Director Traits**: Underperforming directors often exhibited traits like male gender (coefficient for male directors linked to poor outcomes, p=0.000), multiple board seats, and large networks, indicating homophily biases in selection.  
- **Governance Quality Matters**: Firms with weaker governance (E-index coefficient: 0.239*** in probit models) and co-opted boards were more likely to appoint predictably poor directors, highlighting agency-driven selection issues.
